1The Academic English Toolkit — Overview

Academic writing is not simply correct writing — it is writing that operates at a specific register, follows recognisable conventions, and signals intellectual credibility to the reader. These five tools work together to achieve that effect.

2Punctuation — The Core Rules

Poor punctuation is one of the most common reasons academic writing loses marks. The issues are rarely exotic — they are almost always the same six mistakes, made repeatedly. Master these six rules and your writing immediately reads as more competent.

Why punctuation matters in academic writing

Punctuation does not simply separate words — it tells the reader how to group ideas, where one thought ends and another begins, and what kind of relationship exists between clauses. In academic English, punctuation errors signal carelessness, which undermines the credibility of your argument regardless of how strong the content is.

A–ZCapital Letters
Use at the start of every sentence and for proper nouns: names of people, places, languages, institutions, journal titles. Journal of Nutrition, not journal of nutrition.
.Full Stops
End every complete sentence. A sentence without a full stop, or two complete sentences joined by only a comma, is a grammatical error known as a run-on sentence or comma splice.
,Commas
Use after introductory phrases, around non-restrictive clauses, and in lists. A non-restrictive clause gives extra information: One study, published in 2022, found…
'Apostrophes
Use for possession only (the study's findings). Never use contractions in academic writing. don't → do not. it's → it is. No exceptions outside direct quotes.
;Semi-colons
Join two complete, related sentences: [sentence]; however, [sentence]. Also separate complex list items that themselves contain commas.
" "Quotation Marks
Reserve for direct quotes from sources only. Always pair with an in-text citation: (Morris, 2024, p. 3). For short quotes, integrate into your sentence.

The most common error: the comma splice

A comma splice occurs when two complete sentences are joined by a comma alone. It is one of the most frequent punctuation errors in student academic writing.

Comma splice — incorrect
The results were positive, however more research is needed.
Corrected — semi-colon + however
The results were positive; however, more research is needed.
The "however" rule: When "however" connects two complete sentences, it always needs a semi-colon before it and a comma after it. A comma alone before "however" is a comma splice — a grammatical error.

Worked example — full punctuation correction

Worked example · Task 1
Original
plant based diets have become increasingly popular in recent years some researchers suggest that they may reduce the risk of heart disease however its important to note that not all plant based foods are equally healthy for example processed vegan products can contain high levels of sugar and salt one study published in the journal of nutrition 2022 found that people who followed a whole food plant based diet experienced significant improvements in their cardiovascular health
Corrected
Plant-based diets have become increasingly popular in recent years. Some researchers suggest that they may reduce the risk of heart disease; however, it is important to note that not all plant-based foods are equally healthy. For example, processed vegan products can contain high levels of sugar and salt. One study, published in the Journal of Nutrition (2022), found that people who followed a whole-food, plant-based diet experienced significant improvements in their cardiovascular health.
Changes
Capital letters: Plant-based (sentence start), Journal of Nutrition (proper noun), For example (sentence start), One study (sentence start)
Full stops: After "years", "healthy", "salt"
Semi-colon: Before "however" (joining two complete sentences)
Comma: After "however", after "For example", around "published in…2022", after "whole-food"
Apostrophe: "its" → "it is" (contraction removed)
Hyphen: "plant-based" as a compound adjective
💡

Practical tip

Read your writing aloud. Your ear will catch comma splices, missing full stops, and awkward sentence length better than your eye will when reading silently. Every sentence should sound like a complete thought.

3Linking Words — Signposting Your Logic

Linking words (also called connectives or discourse markers) tell the reader how each sentence relates to the one before it. Without them, a paragraph is a list of separate facts. With them, it becomes a structured argument. In academic writing, the logical connection between ideas must always be made explicit — the reader should never have to guess.

The four categories

CategoryWhat it signalsExamplesAvoid in academic writing
Addition Adds a further point in the same direction Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Additionally, As well as, Not only… but also Also (at sentence start) — too informal
Contrast Introduces an opposing or qualifying point However, Although, Nevertheless, Despite, On the other hand, In contrast, Nonetheless But (at sentence start) — too informal
Example Introduces an illustration of the preceding point For example, For instance, Such as, Including, Namely, In particular Like (in this sense) — too conversational
Cause & Result Shows that one thing causes or follows from another Because of, Due to, As a result, Therefore, Consequently, This led to, Hence So (alone) — too informal

Position and punctuation of linking words

The position of a linking word changes its punctuation requirements. This is a common area of error.

Incorrect — comma splice
The results were positive, however more research is needed.
Correct — semi-colon at sentence boundary
The results were positive; however, more research is needed.
Also correct — new sentence
The results were positive. However, more research is needed.
Never write: "However, but…" or "But, however…" These are redundant — both signal contrast. Use one or the other, never both together.

Worked example — adding linking words

Worked example · Task 2
Paragraph
The use of pesticides in modern agriculture has been widely criticised by environmental scientists. However, farmers argue that without them, crop yields would decrease significantly, leading to food shortages. Moreover, studies have shown that certain pesticides may persist in the soil for decades, causing long-term damage to ecosystems. Additionally, there is growing evidence that some pesticide residues enter the food chain, raising concerns about human health. As a result, many governments have begun introducing regulations to limit the use of the most harmful chemicals.
Analysis
However — contrast: scientists criticise pesticides, but farmers defend them.
Moreover — addition: a further piece of negative evidence is added.
Additionally — addition: yet another piece of evidence in the same direction.
As a result — cause/result: the accumulated evidence leads to government action.
Why not "But" or "Also"? These are too informal for academic writing. They lack the register required in a research report or essay.

4Language of Caution — Hedging

Hedging is one of the defining features of academic writing. A hedge is a linguistic device that limits the strength of a claim — it signals to the reader that the evidence supports the claim to a degree, rather than absolutely. Academic knowledge is inherently provisional: new research can always revise or overturn a current consensus.

Why academic writing hedges

When you write without hedging, you make claims that go beyond what your evidence can support. This is not only intellectually dishonest — it also makes you vulnerable to challenge. A skilled reader will immediately ask: "Is this true in every case? Has this been proven definitively?" If the answer is no, an unhedged claim undermines the credibility of your entire argument.

🎓

The core principle

Hedging is not weakness — it is intellectual honesty. You are a scholar, not a salesperson. Your job is to represent what the evidence says, not to sell a conclusion.

Four categories of hedging language

TypeExamplesWhat they do
Modal verbs may, might, could, would, should Express possibility rather than certainty
Hedge verbs seem to, appear to, tend to, suggest, indicate Soften the directness of the assertion — the data "suggests" rather than "proves"
Adverbs possibly, probably, generally, usually, often, rarely, typically Limit scope — something is true "generally" not universally
Limiting phrases in some cases, in many instances, it is likely that…, to a certain extent, in certain contexts Restrict the claim to specific circumstances rather than universal application

Over-hedging: the opposite error

While under-hedging is the more common problem, over-hedging is also a mistake. If a claim is hedged so heavily that it says almost nothing, it contributes nothing to the argument. Compare:

Under-hedged — too strong
Renewable energy will solve climate change.
Over-hedged — too weak
Renewable energy might possibly perhaps be able to help in some situations sometimes.
Correctly hedged — calibrated
Renewable energy may represent one of the most promising pathways to significantly reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

Worked examples — Task 3

Worked example · Task 3
Sentence 1
Original: Renewable energy is the solution to climate change.
Hedged: Renewable energy may represent one of the most promising solutions to climate change.
Hedge used: "may represent" (modal + hedge verb). "Is the solution" is absolute; "may represent one of" limits the claim to possibility and partial scope.
Sentence 2
Original: Social media causes depression in teenagers.
Hedged: Social media appears to be associated with higher rates of depression among some adolescents.
Hedges used: "appears to be associated with" (avoids direct causation), "some" (avoids universality). "Causes" implies a definitive causal relationship that the research does not fully support.
Sentence 3
Original: Countries that invest in education have better economies.
Hedged: Countries that invest in education tend to demonstrate stronger economic performance in many cases.
Hedges used: "tend to" (pattern, not law), "in many cases" (not all). The original implies every country, always. The hedged version reflects the reality of correlation research.

5Active / Passive Voice & Impersonal Writing

Academic writing in most disciplines avoids first-person pronouns (I, we, you) because the focus should be on the research and evidence, not on the researcher. Two main strategies achieve this: the passive voice and impersonal "it" structures.

Understanding the passive voice

In an active sentence, the subject performs the action. In a passive sentence, the subject receives the action — or the actor is removed entirely.

ActivePassive
We conducted a survey of 200 participants.A survey of 200 participants was conducted.
I collected the data over six months.The data were collected over six months.
Some people argue that…It is argued that…
The researchers found that…It was found that… / The findings suggest that…
Note on "data": "Data" is technically plural (singular: datum). In formal academic writing, use "The data were collected…" not "The data was…". Some style guides now accept the singular, but the plural is safer in formal academic contexts.

Impersonal "It" structures

A second strategy is to restructure the sentence so it begins with the impersonal "it" as a grammatical subject, rather than a human actor.

Personal / ActiveImpersonal structure
Many scientists think that…It is widely thought that…
Researchers have found that…It has been found that…
I argue that…It can be argued that…
This shows that…It is suggested by this that… / This suggests that…
⚠️

Field conventions

In some research traditions (especially in the sciences and social sciences), "we" is acceptable in methodology sections to describe the researchers' own actions. Always check the conventions of your specific discipline. For PG01 reports, avoid first person throughout unless your topic area has a clear convention otherwise — check with your tutor.

Worked examples — Task 4

Worked example · Task 4
Sentence 1
Before: I think that sustainable agriculture could solve the problem of food insecurity.
After: It can be argued that sustainable agriculture may offer a solution to the problem of food insecurity.
Note: "I think" → impersonal structure. "Could solve" → "may offer" (also adds hedging). "The problem of" is kept as it is already formal.
Sentence 2
Before: We carried out a review of ten peer-reviewed studies on urban farming.
After: A review of ten peer-reviewed studies on urban farming was carried out.
Note: "We" removed entirely; "carried out" becomes the passive "was carried out". The sentence is restructured to put the object (review) first.
Sentence 3
Before: You can see from the graph that coffee prices have risen sharply.
After: As the graph illustrates, coffee prices have risen sharply in recent years.
Note: "You can see" removed — the graph is now the active agent ("illustrates"), or the phrase can be restructured without any personal reference.
Sentence 4
Before: I believe the findings show a clear link between diet and health outcomes.
After: The findings suggest a clear link between diet and health outcomes.
Note: "I believe" removed. "Show" → "suggest" (also adds hedging — the findings suggest a link, not definitively prove it).

6Putting It All Together — Self-Editing Checklist

Use this checklist every time you finish a paragraph or a complete draft. Go sentence by sentence for maximum effect.

CheckQuestion to askCommon error
01 Punctuation Does every sentence end with a full stop? Are there any contractions? Are commas placed correctly around embedded clauses? Comma before "however", apostrophe in "its" (= it is), run-on sentences
02 Linking Words Is the logical relationship between each sentence made explicit? Is the linking word formal enough for academic writing? "Also" at sentence start, "But" in formal writing, missing linker between paragraphs
03 Hedging Are there any absolute claims that could be challenged? Does every claim accurately reflect the strength of the evidence? "proves", "shows", "will", "all", "every" — replace with hedged equivalents
04 Personal Pronouns Have I removed all instances of I, we, you from my own writing (outside direct quotes)? "I think", "we found", "you can see" — restructure as impersonal
05 Register Does every sentence sound like it belongs in a formal academic report? Any slang, contractions, or conversational phrases? Mixed register (formal paragraph with one casual phrase), exclamation marks, rhetorical questions
