Building an Argument from the Literature

How to find evidence you can trust, turn it into a real argument, and make that argument strong enough to survive a sceptical reader. Read this before class, and again while you draft Assessment 1.

Why this matters now. Assessment 1 (the written report) is due in Week 8. Every grade band on the rubric mentions "argument". A report that simply lists facts will not score well, however accurate those facts are. This chapter is the bridge between having sources and making an argument.

Part 1 — Where good evidence lives

Not all sources are equal. Academic evidence sits in three layers, and knowing which layer you are reading tells you how much weight it can carry.

LayerWhat it isExamples
PrimaryOriginal data, reported for the first time.Peer-reviewed studies, conference papers, patents, technical reports.
SecondaryInterprets and synthesises primary work.Review articles, meta-analyses, systematic reviews, textbooks.
TertiarySummarises secondary sources.Encyclopaedias, dictionaries, handbooks, Wikipedia.
For your report: build on primary and secondary sources. Tertiary sources are useful to orient yourself, but never cite Wikipedia as evidence — follow its reference list down to the primary source and cite that instead.

Part 2 — Searching like a researcher

Typing a whole sentence into a search bar is the slowest way to find good evidence. Four moves make the difference:

1. Keywords, not sentences

Pull the 2–4 concept words out of your question. Add synonyms. Drop filler words ("the", "effect of", "is it true that").

2. Boolean operators

"remote work" OR "hybrid work"   → widens the net
productivity AND employees   → narrows to both terms
productivity NOT student   → excludes a meaning you don't want
productiv*   → truncation catches productivity, productive, productiveness

3. Filter the results

Filter by date (is recency important for this topic?), document type (peer-reviewed?), and relevance. Read the abstract and conclusion before committing to the whole paper.

4. Citation chaining

This is the move most students never use. Backward chaining: look at who this paper cited. Forward chaining: look at who has cited this paper since (Google Scholar's "Cited by"). One good source quickly becomes ten.

Engineering, ICT & Computing databases worth knowing: IEEE Xplore, ACM Digital Library, ScienceDirect. Use your library's access rather than the open web where you can.

Part 3 — CRAAP: judging any source

Before a source goes anywhere near your report, run it through five questions.

Currency
When was it published? Is it current enough for this topic?
Relevance
Does it actually address your question, at the right level?
Authority
Who wrote it? What are their credentials? Who published it?
Accuracy
Is it evidence-based, peer-reviewed, verifiable elsewhere?
Purpose
Why does the source exist — to inform, persuade, or sell? Is there bias?
Rule of thumb: a source that fails Authority or Accuracy is a liability in the marking. An older source can still pass Currency if it is a foundational study — judgement, not a checklist.

Part 4 — Claim, Evidence, Warrant

An argument is not a fact. It is a claim you want the reader to accept, supported by evidence, joined by a warrant — the reasoning that connects the two.

PartWhat it does
ClaimThe point you want the reader to accept.
EvidenceThe sourced information that supports it.
WarrantThe reasoning that links evidence to claim. Usually the missing piece.

Consider this weak argument:

"Students should sleep more. A 2022 study found teenagers who slept eight hours scored higher on memory tests. Therefore, schools should start later."

It has a claim and evidence, but the warrant is unstated and shaky. A memory-test correlation does not by itself establish that later start times are the right policy lever, nor that the effect generalises. The argument "doesn't argue" because the link is missing. Writing the warrant out is the single highest-value move you can make in Assessment 1.

Part 5 — Evidence has two temperatures

TypeHow to handle itExample
UncontestedA fact with no serious dispute. State it directly."Down's syndrome is caused by an extra copy of chromosome 21."
ContestedEvidence exists, but so does counter-evidence. Hedge it."Some sex-linked genes have been associated with aggression" — not "cause".
Hedging toolkit: may · might · could · suggests · appears to · tends to · is associated with · the evidence indicates · it is likely that · in many cases.

Writing "X causes Y" from a single correlational study damages your credibility. Match the strength of your language to the strength of your evidence.

Part 6 — Surviving an objection

A strong argument shows it has considered the other side. Use the three-step move:

StepWhat you doUseful phrases
AcknowledgeName the strongest objection fairly."It is important to note that…", "Critics argue that…"
RefuteExplain why it doesn't overturn your claim."However…", "Nonetheless…", "This overlooks…"
ReinforceRestate your claim, now stronger."Therefore, the evidence still suggests…"
Assessment 1 note: the brief asks "to what extent…" and the rubric mentions "argument" at every grade band. Even though the brief never says the word "counterargument", this Acknowledge–Refute–Reinforce move is expected — operating at paragraph level inside your body sections.

Worked example — the whole thing assembled

CLAIM Remote working arrangements can improve employee productivity.

EVIDENCE A 2024 meta-analysis of 12,000 hybrid workers found a small but consistent rise in measured output (Gomez & Schmidt, 2024).

WARRANT Because the gain held across multiple firms and job types, it is unlikely to be an artefact of one workplace — the arrangement itself appears to be doing the work.

ACKNOWLEDGE It is important to note that the same study reported weaker effects for highly collaborative roles.

REFUTE However, this reflects a need for better-designed collaboration tools rather than a flaw in remote work itself.

REINFORCE The evidence therefore suggests that, with the right support, remote arrangements tend to raise productivity overall.

Notice: the warrant is written out (not assumed); the language is hedged because the evidence is contested ("can", "appears", "tend to"); the objection is the study's own limitation, so it is honest rather than invented; and the single citation is integrated into the sentence, not dropped in raw.

Three frameworks to carry forward: CRAAP to judge a source · Claim–Evidence–Warrant to build · Acknowledge–Refute–Reinforce to defend. Together they take you from a blank page to a paragraph a critical reader cannot knock over.
