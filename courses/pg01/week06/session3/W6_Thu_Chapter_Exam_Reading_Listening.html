Reading & Listening Under Exam Conditions

The receptive skills behind Assessment 3 — how to read a dense text for comprehension, and how to take notes from a talk you hear only once. Practised here on the theme of sustainable farming.

What Assessment 3 tests. The final exam (20%) checks reading and listening comprehension. Unlike your report, you cannot research or revise on the spot — you work from what you read and hear in the moment. These strategies are the difference between understanding a text and merely recognising the words.

Part 1 — Active reading for comprehension

Comprehension questions rarely ask you to repeat the text word-for-word. They ask you to understand it and answer in your own words. Three habits make this reliable:

1. Read with the questions in mind

Skim the questions first, then read. You read differently when you know what you are looking for. Today's text has four threads — environment, cost, health, and diet/access — and the questions track them.

2. Mark as you go

Underline the sentence that answers each question. Put a question mark beside anything you don't follow. This turns a wall of text into a map.

3. Answer by paraphrasing

Re-state the idea in your own words. Copying the text verbatim shows recognition, not comprehension — and in academic work it edges toward the integrity problems covered earlier in the course.

Credibility link: the first comprehension question asks whether the source is credible. This is the same CRAAP test you used on Wednesday — UNEP is an authoritative UN body (Authority), 2024 is current (Currency), and it exists to inform rather than sell (Purpose). Use the vocabulary.

Part 2 — Note-taking from a single listening

In the exam you hear the audio a limited number of times. Trying to transcribe every word means you miss the next sentence. Strong note-takers do the opposite.

Listen for structure, not every word

Most talks are organised around signposts. Today's TED talk moves through eight: the beginning of farming, the first agricultural revolution, technology in crop growing, Costa Rica and the US, rice production, Zambia, infrastructure and storage in India, and the future of farming. Knowing the signposts in advance lets you slot details under headings instead of scrambling.

DoDon't
Listen more than you write on the first pass.Try to write full sentences while listening.
Leave gaps to fill on the second pass.Panic if you miss a point — catch it next time.
Use abbreviations and symbols.Spell every word out.
Use on-screen visuals as memory cues.Ignore the slides and look only at your page.

Short answers vs long answers

Short answers (multiple choice, matching, true/false, fill-in) test whether you caught specific details — your notes should supply them. Long answers (40–70 words) test whether you understood the bigger point. Write in complete sentences and paraphrase the speaker.

Part 3 — What "full marks" looks like

Long answers are marked on a five-band scale. Knowing it before you write tells you exactly what to aim for.

BandDescription
5All key points, accurate and succinct, error-free.
4All key points accurate; minor details may be missing; occasional minor errors.
3Most key points with some accuracy.
2At least two key points with some accuracy.
1Attempted, but no key points and frequent errors make comprehension difficult.
Plainly: Band 5 means every key point, said briefly, with no language errors. Aim to identify the key points first (from your notes), then write them cleanly. Padding does not raise your band; completeness and accuracy do.

Part 4 — Connecting it back to your own writing

Today you analyse someone else's argument; on Wednesday you built your own. They are the same critical muscle. As you read the UNEP text and hear the TED talk, ask the questions from Wednesday:

  • What is the claim, and what evidence supports it?
  • Is the warrant stated, or am I being asked to take a leap?
  • Is a piece of evidence contested (e.g. the health effects of genetic modification) or uncontested?
Carry it forward: a strong sustainable-farming idea from today could become real evidence in an academic argument later. Note which ideas are well-established and which are still debated — that distinction decides whether you state them directly or hedge them.
