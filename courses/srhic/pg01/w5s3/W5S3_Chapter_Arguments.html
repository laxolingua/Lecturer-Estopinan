1What is an Academic Argument?

In everyday language, an argument is a disagreement. In academic writing, an argument is something entirely different: a reasoned, evidence-based case for a position. Academic arguments are not won by speaking louder or more confidently — they are won by the quality of the evidence and the logic connecting that evidence to the claim.

📐

Definition

An academic argument is a structured discourse in which a writer advances a debatable claim, supports it with credible evidence, and explicitly connects the evidence to the claim through logical reasoning (the warrant).

The three components: Claim · Evidence · Warrant

01 Claim
The main point you are arguing. Must be specific, debatable, and supportable. A claim is a position — not a fact and not a topic.
✓ Plant-based diets represent a more sustainable dietary choice for reducing carbon emissions.
✗ Plant-based diets exist. (fact, not claim)
02 Evidence
Data, research, statistics, expert opinion, or verified examples that support the claim. Must be from credible, cited sources.
✓ Scarborough et al. (2023) found that vegan diets produce 75% less carbon than high-meat diets.
✗ I read somewhere that vegan diets are better.
03 Warrant
The logical connection between the evidence and the claim. Often implicit in everyday speech — in academic writing it should be stated explicitly.
✓ Dietary patterns are a significant driver of personal carbon footprints, making food choice a lever for climate action.
✗ (no warrant — leaving the evidence to "speak for itself")
📋

Assessment 1 connection

Every body paragraph in your 1,000-word report should contain all three components. The Claim goes in your topic sentence. The Evidence follows with a signal phrase and citation. The Warrant is the analytical sentence that explains what the evidence means. Missing any one of the three weakens the paragraph.

2Types of Argument — Logos, Ethos, Pathos

Aristotle identified three fundamental modes of persuasion. Understanding which mode you are using — and which is appropriate — is a core academic writing skill.

Logos — Logic & Evidence
Arguments based on data, statistics, research findings, and logical reasoning. The dominant mode in academic writing. Requires cited sources and explicit logical connections.
✓ Battery storage costs have fallen 89% since 2013 (BloombergNEF, 2023), making renewable-plus-storage systems viable.
Ethos — Credibility
Arguments that establish the authority of the source. Citing the WHO, peer-reviewed journals, or recognised domain experts is Ethos in academic writing.
✓ As the IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report (2023) concludes…
✗ My colleague in the energy industry told me…
Pathos — Emotion & Values
Appeals to the reader's emotions, values, or moral instincts. Used sparingly in academic writing. Acceptable when grounded in data; not acceptable as a substitute for evidence.
✓ The 40 million people living in flood-prone coastal areas face existential risk (IPCC, 2023) — a figure that demands policy attention.
✗ We must act NOW before our planet is destroyed!
💡

Key principle

Academic writing is not purely emotionless — but every appeal to values or consequence must be anchored to data. Pathos without Logos is rhetoric. Logos without Ethos (credible sources) is weak. The most powerful academic arguments combine all three, with Logos dominant.

3Causal and Comparative Arguments

Causal arguments

A causal argument claims that one event or condition causes another. These are extremely common in academic writing — and extremely easy to do incorrectly.

⚠️

Correlation ≠ Causation

The most important rule in causal arguments. Two things can increase or decrease together (correlation) without one causing the other. Ice cream sales and drowning rates both rise in summer — but ice cream does not cause drowning. A good causal argument must explain the mechanism: not just that A and B are related, but how A produces B.

Weak causal — correlation only
After the introduction of carbon pricing in Sweden, emissions fell. Therefore, carbon pricing reduces emissions.
Strong causal — mechanism explained
Sweden's carbon tax directly increased the cost of fossil-fuel use, incentivising households and industry to switch to lower-carbon alternatives. This price signal mechanism drove a 26% reduction in emissions between 1991 and 2020 (Swedish EPA, 2022), while GDP grew by 78%, demonstrating that economic performance and emissions reduction are not mutually exclusive.

Comparative arguments

A comparative argument claims that one option is better, more effective, or more appropriate than another. The critical requirement is that you compare on consistent criteria. Comparing X on one criterion and Y on a different criterion is not a fair comparison.

Inconsistent criteria — unfair comparison
"Nuclear power is too expensive, while solar energy is clean." (Comparing on different dimensions — cost vs. environmental impact.)
Consistent criteria — fair comparison
"When evaluated on both deployment speed and lifecycle carbon emissions, solar power outperforms nuclear on both dimensions: solar installations can be completed in 1–2 years versus 10–15 for nuclear, and lifecycle emissions are 6–10x lower (IPCC, 2023)."

4Counterarguments — The Three-Step Move

Including a counterargument in your writing does not weaken your position — it strengthens it. A writer who ignores the strongest objection to their claim looks naive. A writer who acknowledges it fairly and refutes it with evidence looks intellectually rigorous.

🎓

Why include counterarguments?

Academic writing requires balanced, evidence-based reasoning. Readers — and markers — will ask: "But what about the other side?" Anticipating and addressing that question demonstrates critical thinking, which is explicitly assessed in Assessment 1 under Content and Critical Analysis.

The three-step structure

1
Acknowledge — State the opposing view fairly
Represent the opposing position accurately. Do not distort or exaggerate it (that would be a Straw Man fallacy). Use hedged reporting language to signal you are presenting another view, not your own.
  • It could be argued that…
  • Some researchers contend that…
  • Critics of this position suggest that…
  • While it is true that…
2
Refute — Challenge with evidence or by limiting scope
Show why your position is stronger — using evidence, logic, or by restricting the opposing view to specific contexts where it applies but showing it does not apply universally.
  • However, this view fails to account for…
  • While this may be true in specific contexts, the evidence suggests…
  • Nevertheless, the weight of research indicates…
  • This argument, while valid locally, does not undermine the overall picture.
3
Reinforce — Bring the reader back to your claim
After addressing the opposition, restate your claim — now stronger for having survived challenge. Link back to the evidence you presented earlier.
  • This therefore reinforces the argument that…
  • The balance of evidence thus supports the claim that…
  • Taken together, these points confirm that…
  • It therefore follows that…

Worked example — full three-step

Worked example · Topic: Plant-based diets and sustainability
ACKNOWLEDGE
It could be argued that promoting plant-based diets as a climate solution oversimplifies a complex issue. As Garnett (2020) notes, the environmental impact of food production varies considerably depending on farming practices, land use, and transportation distance, meaning that locally-sourced animal products can, in some cases, have a lower carbon footprint than imported plant-based alternatives.
REFUTE
However, this argument, while valid in specific local contexts, does not undermine the overall picture. The global average data consistently demonstrates that plant-based diets produce substantially lower emissions at scale. Poore and Nemecek (2018), analysing over 38,000 farms in 119 countries, found that a vegan diet reduces food-related emissions by an average of 73% compared to a high-meat diet.
REINFORCE
This therefore reinforces the argument that, at a population level, dietary shifts towards plant-based food represent one of the most impactful individual actions available for reducing carbon footprints. Local exceptions do not negate the structural pattern.
Notice: Orange highlighting = hedging in the Acknowledge step. Green highlighting = contrast and reinforce language. The concession phrase "while valid in specific local contexts" admits the opposing point has some validity before showing it does not apply universally.

5Logical Fallacies

A logical fallacy is an error in reasoning that makes an argument appear valid when it is not. Recognising these in other people's writing — and eliminating them from your own — is a mark of academic maturity.

Ad Hominem
Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself.
"We shouldn't listen to his climate views — he's not even a scientist."
Straw Man
Misrepresenting the opposing position to make it easier to attack.
"People who support renewable energy want us all to live without electricity."
Slippery Slope
Claiming one event will inevitably lead to extreme consequences without evidence.
"If we allow this one regulation, it will lead to total government control."
False Dichotomy
Presenting only two options when more exist.
"You're either with us or against us — there is no middle ground."
Post Hoc
Claiming that because B followed A, A caused B.
"After the new policy, crime fell. Therefore the policy reduced crime."
Appeal to False Authority
Citing an authority outside their area of expertise.
"This famous actor says the vaccine is unsafe."

6Source Credibility — The CRAAP Test

The strength of an academic argument depends entirely on the quality of its evidence. The CRAAP test (Blakeslee, 2004) is a five-criterion framework used by academic librarians worldwide to evaluate source quality.

C
Currency
Is the source recent enough? Science and policy change quickly. A 2005 study on AI is not current.
R
Relevance
Does it directly address your question? A study on diet in Finland may not apply to Germany.
A
Authority
Is the author an expert in this specific field? Is it peer-reviewed? Published in a reputable journal?
A
Accuracy
Can the claims be verified? Are sources cited? Does it align with the broader literature?
P
Purpose
Why was this written? To inform, persuade, or sell? Industry-funded research may carry bias.

Note on Wikipedia: Wikipedia is useful for orientation and for following up its own reference lists, but it is never an acceptable primary source in academic writing. Cite the original studies that Wikipedia references, not Wikipedia itself.

7The Argument Paragraph Template

This five-step template maps directly onto the Assessment 1 marking rubric. Every body paragraph in your report should follow this structure.

StepWhat to writeExample
Topic SentenceYour mini-claim for this paragraph. One clear, specific, debatable sentence.Plant-based diets represent a more sustainable dietary option than meat-based alternatives.
EvidenceIntroduce evidence with a signal phrase. Include in-text citation.According to Scarborough et al. (2023), vegan diets produce on average 75% less carbon than high-meat diets.
Analysis (Warrant)Explain what the evidence means — connect it to the claim. Never let evidence speak for itself.This significant differential suggests that individual dietary choices can have a measurable impact on carbon footprints at scale.
CounterargumentAcknowledge the strongest objection in one sentence. Refute in one sentence.While some critics note that local animal products can outperform imported plant-based foods, the global average data consistently supports dietary shift as an effective intervention.
Concluding SentenceReinforce the claim and signal the link to the next paragraph.The evidence therefore supports the contention that promoting plant-based diets represents a viable climate strategy at both individual and policy levels.

8Model Answers — Case Studies

Case Study 1 — Renewable Energy Argument Analysis

Model answer
CLAIM
Intermittency does not represent an insurmountable obstacle to a renewable energy transition.
EVIDENCE
BloombergNEF (2023): battery storage costs fallen 89% since 2013. Tesla Megapack and grid-scale lithium-ion systems cited as concrete examples.
WARRANT
Falling costs make large-scale storage deployment increasingly viable, which directly resolves the intermittency problem at scale.
ARISTOTLE
Dominant = Logos (statistical data, named report). Secondary = Ethos (BloombergNEF as recognised authority, Tesla as established technology). No Pathos — appropriate for a technical argument.
3-STEP
Acknowledge: "Critics argue that without large-scale energy storage…" — Refute: "Nevertheless, recent advances… suggest this barrier is being rapidly addressed." — Reinforce: "This evidence therefore supports the contention that intermittency… does not represent an insurmountable obstacle."
